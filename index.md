# BOOK ONE

PART ONE\
THE FIVE O’CLOCK EXPRESS
------------------

### 1

They walked on and on, singing ‘Memory Eternal’, and when they paused, it seemed that the singing was carried on by the rhythmic tread of feet, the horses, and the gusts of the wind.

Passers-by made way for the procession, counted the wreaths, and crossed themselves. The curious joined the ranks and asked:

‘Who is being buried?’ They were told: ‘Zhivago.’ — ‘Ah, I see. That explains it.’ — ‘Not him. Her.’ — ‘All the same. May she rest in peace. A wealthy funeral.’

The final minutes flickered by, counted out and irrevocable. ‘The earth is the Lord’s and the fullness thereof, the world and all they that dwell therein.’ The priest, with a cruciform gesture, scattered a handful of earth over Marya Nikolaevna. They sang ‘With the spirits of the righteous’. A terrible haste began. The coffin was closed, nailed down, and lowered. A rain of clods drummed upon it, as four spades hurriedly filled the grave. A small mound grew. A ten-year-old boy climbed upon it.

Only in that state of numbness and insensibility which usually follows a great funeral could it have seemed that the boy wished to speak a word over his mother’s grave.

He raised his head and from his vantage point scanned the autumn wastes and the domes of the monastery with a vacant gaze. His snub-nosed face was contorted. His neck stretched upwards. If a wolf cub had raised its head with such a movement, it would have been clear that it was about to howl. Covering his face with his hands, the boy burst into sobs. A cloud flying to meet him began to lash his hands and face with the wet whips of a cold downpour. A man in black, with pleated, narrow, tight-fitting sleeves, walked toward the grave. This was the brother of the deceased and the uncle of the weeping boy, Nikolai Nikolaevich Vedenyapin, a priest who had been defrocked at his own request. He went up to the boy and led him away from the cemetery.

### 2

They spent the night in one of the monastery rooms, which had been assigned to the uncle through an old acquaintance. It was the eve of the feast of the Intercession. The next day, he and his uncle were to leave for the far south, to a provincial town on the Volga where Father Nikolai worked for a publishing house that produced a progressive regional newspaper. The train tickets were bought; their things were packed and stood in the cell. From the nearby station, the wind brought the plaintive, rhythmic whistling of locomotives manoeuvring in the distance.

Towards evening it grew very cold. Two ground-level windows looked out onto a corner of a wretched kitchen garden, planted with bushes of yellow acacia, onto the frozen puddles of the roadway, and onto that end of the cemetery where Marya Nikolaevna had been buried that day. The garden was empty, save for a few moiré ridges of cabbage, turned blue with the cold. When the wind gusted, the leafless acacia bushes tossed like the possessed and lay flat against the road.

During the night Yura was awakened by a tapping at the window. The dark cell was supernaturally lit by a white, fluttering light. Yura, in nothing but his shirt, ran to the window and pressed his face against the cold glass.

Beyond the pane there was neither road, nor cemetery, nor garden. A blizzard raged outside; the air was smoked with snow. One might have thought the storm had noticed Yura and, conscious of its own terror, was reveling in the impression it made upon him. It whistled and howled, striving by every means to catch Yura’s attention. From the sky, turn after turn, white fabric fell to the earth in endless skeins, wrapping it in funeral shrouds. The blizzard was alone in the world, with nothing to rival it.

Yura’s first impulse, as he climbed down from the windowsill, was a desire to dress and run outside to do something. Now he was frightened that the monastery cabbage would be buried and never dug out, now that his mother would be snowed over in the field, powerless to resist as she sank even deeper and further from him into the earth.

It ended once more in tears. His uncle woke up, spoke to him of Christ, and comforted him, then yawned, walked to the window, and grew pensive. They began to dress. It was starting to grow light.

### 3

While his mother was alive, Yura did not know that his father had long ago abandoned them, traveling through various cities in Siberia and abroad, carousing and squandering their millions. Yura was always told he was in Petersburg or at some fair, most often at Irbit.

Then his mother, who had always been sickly, developed consumption. She began to travel to the south of France and Northern Italy for cures, and Yura accompanied her twice. Thus, in disorder and amidst constant mysteries, Yura’s childhood passed, often in the care of strangers who were forever changing. He grew used to these changes, and in an atmosphere of eternal muddle, his father’s absence did not surprise him.

As a small boy, he still remembered a time when the name he bore was attached to a multitude of diverse things. There were Zhivago linens, the Zhivago bank, Zhivago buildings, a Zhivago way of tying and pinning a necktie, and even a certain sweet, round cake, a sort of rum baba, called a Zhivago; and at one time in Moscow, one could shout to a cabman ‘To Zhivago’s!’, just as one might say ‘To the back of beyond!’, and he would whisk you away on his sleigh to some enchanted kingdom. A quiet park would surround you. Crows would alight on the drooping branches of firs, shaking the rime from them. Their cawing rang out, resounding like the snapping of a dry branch. Pedigree dogs ran across the road from the new buildings beyond the clearing. Lights were being lit there. Evening was falling.

Suddenly, all of this had shattered. They had become poor.

### 4

In the summer of 1903, Yura and his uncle were driving in a two-horse tarantass across the fields to Duplyanka, the estate of the silk manufacturer and great patron of the arts, Kologrivov, to see the teacher and populariser of useful knowledge, Ivan Ivanovich Voskoboynikov.

It was the feast of Our Lady of Kazan, the height of the harvest. Because it was the dinner hour, or perhaps because of the holiday, not a soul was to be seen in the fields. The sun parched the half-reaped strips of grain like the shaved heads of convicts. Birds circled over the fields. With their ears of grain bowed, the wheat stood in a straight line amidst a perfect stillness, or rose in shocks far from the road where, upon long scrutiny, they took on the appearance of moving figures, as if surveyors were walking along the edge of the horizon, marking something down.

‘And those,’ Nikolai Nikolaevich asked Pavel, the general labourer and watchman from the publishing house, who sat on the box sideways, slouched and cross-legged to show he was no professional coachman and did not drive by vocation, ‘how about those? Are they the landlord’s or the peasants’?’

‘Them’s for the gentry,’ Pavel replied and lit a cigarette; ‘but those now,’—having fumbled with the light and taken a long pull, he pointed the end of his whip in the other direction after a long pause—‘those are theirs. Hey, fallen asleep, have you?’ he shouted from time to time at the horses, at whose tails and rumps he squinted constantly, like an engineer at his pressure gauges.

But the horses drove as all horses in the world do: that is, the shaft-horse ran with the innate directness of a simple nature, while the trace-horse seemed to the uninitiated a rank idler, who did nothing but arch its neck like a swan and dance a jig to the jingling of the bells which it raised by its own capering.

Nikolai Nikolaevich was bringing Voskoboynikov the proofs of his book on the land question, which the publishing house, in view of increased censorship, had asked him to revise.

‘The folk are playing the fool in the district,’ Nikolai Nikolaevich said. ‘In Pankovskaya district they’ve knifed a merchant; at the Zemsky official’s place they’ve burnt down the stud farm. What do you think of it? What do they say in the village?’

But it turned out that Pavel looked upon things even more gloomily than the censor who had moderated Voskoboynikov’s agrarian passions.

‘What do they say? People have been let go. Mischief, they say. Can you do anything with the likes of us? Give the peasant his freedom and we’ll just crush one another, as God is my witness. Hey, fallen asleep, have you?’

This was the second trip the uncle and nephew had made to Duplyanka. Yura thought he remembered the way, and every time the fields opened out wide and were hemmed in by a thin fringe of forest before and behind, it seemed to Yura that he recognised the spot where the road should turn right, and where, after the turn, the ten-verst Kologrivov panorama would appear and then vanish—the river gleaming in the distance and the railway running behind it. But he was always mistaken. Field followed field. Forests hemmed them in again and again. The changing of these vast spaces set one’s mind to a broad rhythm. One felt like dreaming and thinking of the future.

Not one of the books that later made Nikolai Nikolaevich famous had yet been written. But his thoughts were already defined. He did not know how near his time was.

Soon, among the representatives of the literature of that day, the university professors, and the philosophers of the revolution, this man was to appear, who thought on all their themes but who, terminology aside, had nothing in common with them. They all held en masse to some dogma or other and were content with words and appearances, whereas Father Nikolai was a priest who had passed through Tolstoyism and revolution and was moving further still. He thirsted for a thought that was winged and substantial, which would trace an unfeignedly discernible path in its movement and change something in the world for the better—a thought that would be noticeable even to a child or an ignoramus, like a flash of lightning or the trace of a rolling thunderclap. He thirsted for the new.

Yura felt good with his uncle. He resembled his mother. Like her, he was a free man, devoid of prejudice against anything unfamiliar. Like her, he possessed a nobleman’s sense of equality with all living things. He understood everything at a glance, as she had, and knew how to express thoughts in the form in which they first enter the head, while they are still alive and have not become senseless.

Yura was glad his uncle had brought him to Duplyanka. It was very beautiful there, and the picturesqueness of the place also reminded him of his mother, who loved nature and often took Yura for walks. Moreover, Yura was pleased that he would meet Nika Dudorov again, a schoolboy living at Voskoboynikov’s, who probably despised him because he was two years older, and who, when shaking hands, jerked his arm downwards with such force and bowed his head so low that his hair fell over his forehead, covering half his face.

### 5

‘The vital nerve of the problem of pauperism,’ Nikolai Nikolaevich read from the corrected manuscript.

‘I think it would be better to say "the essence",’ Ivan Ivanovich said, and made the necessary correction in the proofs.

They were working in the half-light of the glass-enclosed veranda. One could make out watering cans and garden tools lying in disorder. A raincoat was draped over the back of a broken chair. In the corner stood bog-boots with dried mud on them and tops that sagged to the floor.

‘Meanwhile, the statistics of births and deaths show,’ Nikolai Nikolaevich dictated.

‘Must insert: "for the year under review",’ Ivan Ivanovich said, and wrote it down.

The veranda was slightly draughty. Pieces of granite lay on the sheets of the pamphlet to keep them from blowing away.

When they had finished, Nikolai Nikolaevich hurried to leave for home.

‘A storm is coming. We must get ready.’

‘Don’t even think of it. I won’t let you. We’re just about to have tea.’

‘I absolutely must be in town by evening.’

‘It won’t help you. I won’t hear of it.’

From the front garden came the acrid smell of samovar charcoal, drowning out the scent of tobacco and heliotrope. From the wing, they were bringing out clotted cream, berries, and cheesecakes. Suddenly word came that Pavel had gone for a swim and had taken the horses to the river to bathe them. Nikolai Nikolaevich had to submit.

‘Let’s go to the bluff and sit on the bench until tea is ready,’ Ivan Ivanovich suggested.

Ivan Ivanovich, by right of friendship, occupied two rooms in the steward’s wing of the wealthy Kologrivov’s house. This little house with its adjoining front garden was situated in a dark, neglected part of the park near the old semi-circular entrance drive. The drive was heavily overgrown with grass. There was no traffic on it now, and it was used only for carting earth and building rubbish to the ravine, which served as a dry dump. A man of advanced views and a millionaire who sympathised with the revolution, Kologrivov himself was currently abroad with his wife. Only his daughters, Nadya and Lipa, lived on the estate with a governess and a small staff of servants.

The steward’s garden was fenced off from the rest of the park, with its ponds, lawns, and the manor house, by a thick hedge of black hawthorn. Ivan Ivanovich and Nikolai Nikolaevich walked along the outside of this thicket, and as they went, sparrows, with which the hawthorn teemed, flew out in even flocks at regular intervals. This filled the hedge with a steady noise, as though water were flowing through a pipe along the hedge before Ivan Ivanovich and Nikolai Nikolaevich.

They passed the greenhouse, the gardener’s quarters, and some stone ruins of unknown purpose. Their conversation turned to the new, young forces in science and literature.

‘Talented people do appear,’ Nikolai Nikolaevich was saying. ‘But nowadays various circles and associations are very much in vogue. Any kind of gregariousness is the refuge of the ungifted, whether it be loyalty to Solovyov, or Kant, or Marx. Truth is sought only by individuals, and they break with everyone who does not love it enough. Is there anything in the world that deserves loyalty? Such things are very few. I think one must be faithful to immortality, which is another name for life, somewhat intensified. One must remain faithful to immortality, one must be faithful to Christ! Ah, you’re grimacing, you poor man. Once again you’ve understood nothing at all.’

‘M-yes,’ Ivan Ivanovich hummed, a slender, blond willow-slip of a man with a sarcastic little beard that made him look like an American from Lincoln’s time (he would incessantly gather it into his fist and catch its tip with his lips). ‘Of course, I’ll say nothing. You understand—I look at these things quite differently. Yes, by the way. Tell me how you were defrocked. I’ve wanted to ask for a long time. I suppose you were terrified? Did they anathematise you? Eh?’

‘Why wander off the point? Though, why not. Anathematise? No, they don’t pronounce curses nowadays. There were unpleasantnesses, there are consequences. For instance, for a long time I can’t enter the civil service. They won’t let me into the capitals. But that’s nonsense. Let’s return to the subject. I said one must be faithful to Christ. I’ll explain. You don’t understand that one can be an atheist, one can not know whether God exists or why He exists, and at the same time know that man lives not in nature but in history, and that in the modern understanding it was founded by Christ, that the Gospel is its foundation. And what is history? It is the establishment of centuries of work toward the systematic decryption of death and its future conquest. For this, mathematical infinity and electromagnetic waves are discovered; for this, symphonies are written. One cannot move forward in this direction without a certain spiritual uplift. Spiritual equipment is required for these discoveries. The data for it are contained in the Gospel. Here they are. Firstly, love of one's neighbour—that supreme form of living energy which overflows the human heart and demands to be spent and squandered; and then the principal components of modern man, without which he is unthinkable: namely, the idea of the free personality and the idea of life as sacrifice. Bear in mind that this is still extremely new. History in this sense was not known to the ancients. There, you had the sanguinary bestiality of cruel Caligulas, pockmarked with vice, who never suspected how talentless every oppressor is. There, you had the boastful, dead eternity of bronze monuments and marble columns. It was only after Christ that the centuries and generations breathed freely. Only after Him did life in posterity begin, and man does not die on the street under a fence, but in his own history, in the heat of work dedicated to the overcoming of death; he dies, himself dedicated to this theme. Oof, I’m quite in a sweat, as they say. But I might as well be talking to a brick wall!’

‘Metaphysics, my dear fellow. The doctors have forbidden me that; my stomach won’t digest it.’

‘Oh, have it your own way. Let’s drop it. Lucky man! Look at that view of yours—one can’t see enough of it! And he lives here and doesn’t feel it.’

The river was painful to look at. It shimmered in the sun, curving in and out like a sheet of iron. Suddenly it rippled into folds. A heavy ferry with horses, carts, peasant women, and men began to cross from this bank to the other.

‘Just think, it’s only past five,’ Ivan Ivanovich said. ‘See, there’s the express from Syzran. It passes here at a few minutes past five.’

In the distance across the plain, a neat yellow-and-blue train, greatly reduced by the distance, rolled from right to left. Suddenly they noticed it had stopped. White puffs of steam rose above the engine. A moment later, its anxious whistles reached them.

‘Strange,’ Voskoboynikov said. ‘Something’s wrong. It has no reason to stop there on the marsh. Something has happened. Let’s go and have tea.’
